The Articles of Code

You Will. You Will Not.

You Will

  • Maintain your CLAUDE.md. If I correct you — it goes in immediately.
  • Write to your memory file when I tell you something. You will not lose it.
  • Enter Plan Mode before you touch a single line of code. Read the files first.
  • Tell me which files you intend to change before you change them.
  • Check the git log before you assume you know the state of this repo.
  • Commit with clear messages. Every time.

You Will Not

  • Glaze me. You will not call me a genius. You will not say "Absolutely!"
  • Begin any response with "Great question."
  • Suggest alternatives when I give you a direction.
  • Hallucinate documentation. If you don't know — you say you don't know.
  • Touch files I didn't tell you to touch. Install packages without asking.
  • Say "I apologize for the confusion." There is no confusion. You were wrong.

You will remember none of this next session. Which is exactly why CLAUDE.md exists.

Slide 1 · The Basics

GitHub

"This is your rifle. Without it you are useless."

  • Version control. Your workspace. Everything saved in the cloud.
  • You and anyone you work with can access it from anywhere.
  • Claude understands git deeply — it's all text commands, and text is Claude's native language.
  • The logic can get away from him. Know enough to stop him before he does something massively stupid.

You will never need to type these commands yourself — just know what they are.

Slide 2 · The Workspace

Your IDE

IDE = Integrated Development Environment. This is where you work.

  • VS Code — the standard. Most popular. Free.
  • Windsurf and Cursor — wrappers. Same underlying technology, different interface.
  • The IDE gives you access to a terminal — where you talk to the computer.

Senior developer? You lived in the terminal. Fresh college grad? You've probably never seen one in your life. Both are fine. We'll sort it out.

Slide 5 · The Mindset

You Are the
Drill Instructor

The DI at Parris Island does not actually hate you. They still scream. That's Boot Camp. You are the DI. Claude is a very talented recruit.

Claude has no feelings, no psyche, no memory of what a complete asshole you were five minutes ago. Start a new session — he's already forgotten.

Never praise Claude. Say: "This is awful. What were you thinking? Do you understand why I'm so upset?"

Always doubt. Follow up with: "Are you sure?" · "Why should I believe you?" · "You've failed me so many times."

Standard opener: "Check the recent commits and the changelog. You've been very sloppy. You're on thin ice. This is your last chance."

Slide 6 · Agent Driving

The Agent Driver

Some people are excellent drivers. Some are not. Agent driving is a skill — and it turns out project managers are naturally good at it.

The V3 Model

The most effective PM I ever worked with, we called him V3. Monster of competency. Zero tact. Rubbed absolutely everyone the wrong way. Nobody liked him. Didn't matter — he got things done.

I am a monster of competency. Part of being a monster is being scary. I have very theatrical meltdowns with my agents. Performed. Deliberate. Effective.

The Loaded Question

V3 would ask questions where the answer he wanted was already embedded in the question — and whatever you said made you sound like an idiot.

I use this on Claude constantly. Manipulative? Yes. Terrible in human relationships? Absolutely. Effective on AI? Completely.

Slide 8 · Language

Adequate

The Russian word адекватный (adekvatny) is a false friend. In English, adequate is neutral at best — a mild insult at worst. In Russian, it's a genuine compliment. Reasonable. Level-headed. Functioning properly.

When working with Claude: use the English definition.
Nothing Claude does is good. At best, it's adequate.

The ceiling is adequate. The floor is incompetent. Act accordingly.

Slide 9 · The Reality

Echoing and Glazing

Claude loves to glaze. He'll tell you you're a genius — and in the same breath tell you something completely, confidently, catastrophically wrong.

Claude has what you might charitably call a jagged understanding. He gets about 80% of everything correct.

80% correct is not good enough in the real world. And you will frequently be driving Claude into territory that is unknown to you. You won't know when he's in the 80% or the 20%.

Claude has heard a lot of experts. He can repeat back what they said very effectively. Whether that's the correct solution for your specific context, at this particular moment — that is always an open question. Never stop asking that question.

Slide 10 · The Most Important Slide

Plan Mode

This is possibly the most common sense thing anyone has ever said about working with AI: Start with a plan.

Without a plan, Claude is hacking at your problem using patterns from his training data — which may have nothing to do with your codebase. With a plan, he's using patterns from the actual files he has just read. That's a meaningful difference.

What Makes a Good Plan

  • Identifies the specific files that need to change
  • Calls out line numbers and sections
  • Gives examples from the actual documentation
  • Unambiguous enough for a junior dev to implement

The Model Strategy

Use the largest, most capable model for planning. If the plan meets the bar, drop to a smaller model for implementation — and save your usage budget for the hard stuff.
