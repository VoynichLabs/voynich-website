abstract: "Do given names predict adult socioeconomic outcomes, or do observed correlations merely reflect parental status? In 1980s Denmark, university-educated parents were 1.69× more likely to name sons 'Simon,' yet men named Simon earn 17.1% more at age 40. Using Denmark's comprehensive registry data (Statistikbanken), we decompose this raw correlation into confounded and residual components via causal inference. Our analysis reveals that parental education confounds 52% of the raw earnings advantage, while a statistically significant residual effect of 7.9% persists after adjustment (95% CI: 7.4%–8.6%). ## Key Findings

- Danish university-educated parents were **1.69× more likely** to name sons "Simon" in the 1980s
- Men named Simon earn **17.1% more** at age 40 vs. comparison cohort (Lars, Morten, Thomas, Martin, Mikkel)
- **52% of that earnings advantage** is explained by parental education (confounding)
- A **7.9% residual effect persists** after full SES adjustment (CI: 7.4–8.6%)
- Three independent methods (doubly robust matching, domain adaptation, parametric regression) converge on ~8%
- Sensitivity analysis: residual survives modest unmeasured confounding, fragile to large hidden bias

## Methodology

Peak-cohort design: Simon surged in Danish popularity 1975–1995, creating a clean cohort. Compared against age-matched peers with similar names that peaked in the same era. Causal decomposition via doubly robust augmented nearest-neighbor matching, with Rosenbaum bounds and E-value sensitivity analysis.
